Electric Fields


Electric Charge and Electrostatic Force

To understand electric fields, we must first understand how electric charges work. The law of electric charges is thatlike charges repelandopposite charges attract.An object’s electric charge is determined by the amount of electrons it has, whether it be an excess amount or a deficit. Thus, a transfer of electrons to or away from the object is all it takes to determine what kind of charge it has. If electrons are taken away from the object, it is positively charged. On the other hand, if electrons are added to the object, the object is negatively charged.

Another thing to note is how these charges move from one atom to another. We’ve heard of conductors and insulators before, but what are they really?Conductorsare solid materials that can easily move electrons from one object to another. Some examples of conductors are most metals (gold, silver, aluminum). Meanwhileinsulatorsare solid materials in which electrons cannot easily travel from one atom to the other. Glass, cork and plastic are all examples of insulators.

We delved further into this idea of electrostatic forces when doing our pith ball lab in class! Long story short, we learned that the strength of electrostatic force is inversely related to the distance between two objects (which would be the pith balls in this specific case!). As the distance increases, the electrostatic force – attraction or repulsion– between the objects decreases. And vice versa: if the distance between two objects decreases, the electrostatic force between them increases.

Coulomb's Law

Millikan Experiment

The Millikan Experiment was performed originally by Robert Milikan in 1909. It was revolutionary, as it was the first and foremost experiment successful in determining the measure of electric charge of a single electron.

To measure the charge, Millikan came up with the idea of spraying a fine mist of oil from an atomizer, similar to the misting mechanism we would find on perfume bottles. The oil droplets fell through a region between two parallel plates, with opposite electric charges (as you can see on the picture to the right. Due to the opposite charges of the plates, an electric field ensues between them, creating a potential difference between the top and bottom plate. By adjusting the magnitude of the electric field through a series of batteries, Millikan was able to balance the downward gravitational force and bring a charged oil droplet to rest, right between the two plates.

After numerous repetitions of the process of balancing a droplet of oil, measuring it’s voltage, letting it fall and finally–analyzing its speed, Millikan was able to conclude that the charge value equaled the elementary charge of an electron. After being confirmed by other scientists and researchers throughout the years, the currently acceptedelementary charge of an electron is e = 1.602 x 10-19 C.
